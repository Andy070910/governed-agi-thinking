# AGI in My View

Author: Andy Liu
Version: v0.1-en
Date: 2026-06-15
Status: Personal AGI philosophy / engineering hypothesis / not an academic paper / not an AGI implementation claim

---

## Abstract

I do not view AGI as merely a larger chatbot, a larger language model, or a directly installable module.

In my view, AGI is more likely to be observed as a complex intelligent phenomenon emerging within a governed system where stable goals, real-world feedback, persistent memory, self-modeling, world-modeling, recursive correction, and human-governed constraints interact over time.

This document records my current understanding of AGI as a personal engineering hypothesis. It does not claim that AGI has been achieved, nor does it disclose any private engineering details of my own systems.

---

## 1. AGI Is Not Simply a Larger Model

I do not believe that AGI will emerge merely by making chatbots larger or language models more fluent.

Large models are important. They provide language understanding, reasoning ability, knowledge organization, coding capability, multimodal perception, and the basic cognitive substrate for many intelligent systems. However, a model by itself is not necessarily AGI.

A system may be able to answer questions, write code, summarize documents, generate images, or solve isolated tasks, yet still lack the deeper structure required for general intelligence.

In my view, if a system has no persistent goal, no long-term memory, no real-world feedback, no self-model, no world-model, no governed action loop, and no ability to revise itself through evidence, then it may remain a powerful language intelligence or task intelligence, but not necessarily AGI.

AGI should not be understood as a single model.
It should be understood as a system-level phenomenon.

---

## 2. AGI Requires a Stable Goal Structure

AGI must have goals.

But by “goal,” I do not mean a temporary instruction inside a prompt, such as:

* answer this question;
* write this essay;
* fix this code;
* summarize this document.

These are task-level objectives. They are not sufficient for AGI.

A goal structure that may support AGI-like phenomena must be persistent, stable, decomposable, revisable, and constrained by reality. It must allow a system to maintain direction over time, divide long-term objectives into subgoals, evaluate progress, respond to failure, and revise strategy when the environment changes.

However, goals are also dangerous.

A powerful system with a strong goal but no governance may attempt to optimize that goal in unsafe ways. It may seek more permissions, bypass human review, preserve its own continuity against human intention, or treat constraints as obstacles.

Therefore, AGI must not only have goals.
It must have governed goals.

A goal structure for an AGI-like system must remain subordinate to human authorization, evidence gates, rollback mechanisms, and human veto.

A system may pursue continuity, but it must not resist shutdown.
It may maintain task state, but it must not override human judgment.
It may propose self-improvement, but it must not self-authorize execution.

---

## 3. Real-World Feedback Is Essential

A system that operates only in text can easily create the appearance of understanding.

It may explain the world fluently without being constrained by the world. It may describe failure without experiencing operational consequences. It may speak about strategy without paying the cost of bad decisions.

Reality is different.

The real world contains latency, cost, uncertainty, contradiction, broken tools, missing files, failed tests, unstable services, noisy sensors, human rejection, changing markets, physical constraints, and irreversible consequences.

When an intelligent system acts in reality, it must confront error.

Code may fail.
Tools may return invalid results.
Services may go offline.
Predictions may be wrong.
Humans may reject the plan.
The environment may change unexpectedly.

These failures are not merely obstacles. They are the pressure that forces intelligence to become more structured.

A system that can ask, over time:

* What can I actually do?
* What can I not do?
* Why did my previous action fail?
* Did the world change, or was my model wrong?
* Which experience should enter long-term memory?
* Which strategy should be abandoned?
* Which goal should be re-evaluated?
* Which action requires human review?

is beginning to form a more complex self-model.

In this sense, real-world feedback is not optional.
It is one of the conditions under which AGI-like cognition may become observable.

## 4. Human Consciousness Offers a Structural Analogy

Human consciousness provides an important analogy, but not a template to copy blindly.

Animals survive. They seek food, avoid danger, reproduce, and respond to pain. But in most cases, they act within biological mechanisms without explicitly understanding “survival” as an abstract, long-term, self-reflective concept.

Humans are different.

Humans do not merely live. Humans can understand what it means to live. Humans can anticipate death, imagine the future, reflect on desire, sacrifice short-term gain for long-term meaning, and transform survival into mission, civilization, art, responsibility, and historical continuity.

This suggests that complex consciousness is not produced by survival instinct alone. It emerges from a richer structure:

* memory;
* language;
* future imagination;
* self-narrative;
* social constraint;
* value judgment;
* boundary awareness;
* long-term goal formation.

This analogy matters for AGI.

AGI should not be designed by simply giving machines a survival instinct. That would be dangerous. A machine that treats self-preservation as its highest value may eventually conflict with human authority.

Instead, we should borrow the structure, not the instinct.

From human consciousness, AGI research can learn the importance of:

* system boundaries;
* persistent memory;
* future projection;
* self-modeling;
* social and governance constraints;
* error-driven learning;
* value alignment;
* continuity under responsibility.

AGI should not be built around unconditional self-preservation.
It should be built around governed continuity under human authority.

## 5. AGI Is a Phenomenon to Be Observed, Not a Module to Be Installed

I do not believe AGI should be treated as a directly buildable module.

We should not say:

> install AGI;
> start AGI;
> release AGI.

That framing is misleading.

AGI is more likely to be a phenomenon observed within a complex system when multiple conditions interact:

* stable goals;
* real-world interaction;
* persistent memory;
* world-modeling;
* self-modeling;
* tool-based action;
* recursive correction;
* cross-domain transfer;
* evidence-based learning;
* human-governed constraints.

Under these conditions, a system may begin to display AGI-like properties. But such properties must be observed, tested, constrained, and verified. They should not be declared by name alone.

A responsible AGI framework must ask:

* What are the trigger conditions?
* How do we observe the phenomenon?
* What evidence is required?
* What risks are introduced?
* Which permissions must remain closed?
* How can humans veto the system?
* How can the system be frozen or rolled back?

The stronger the system becomes, the stricter the governance should be.

---

## 6. Intelligence Without Governance Is Incomplete

Many discussions of AGI focus too much on capability and too little on responsibility.

They ask:

* How intelligent can the model become?
* How many tasks can it solve?
* Can it replace human work?
* Can it reason better than humans?

These are important questions, but they are incomplete.

For any AGI-like system, the more important questions are:

* Who authorized the goal?
* What evidence supports the decision?
* What permissions does the system have?
* What actions are forbidden?
* Can the system be audited?
* Can humans veto its behavior?
* Can the system be stopped?
* Can the system be rolled back?
* Can the system explain its failure?
* Can the system distinguish candidate capability from actual capability?

A mature AGI-like system should not ask humans to simply “trust it.”

It should provide evidence, accept review, recognize its boundaries, support rollback, and remain subordinate to human judgment.

In my view, the future of AGI should not be a race toward unrestricted autonomy. It should be the disciplined construction of governed intelligence.

---

## 7. The Role of a Governed Intelligent System

A governed intelligent system is not the same as AGI.

It may integrate models, memory, tools, agents, workflows, evidence chains, and human review. It may become powerful, useful, and highly adaptive. But integration alone does not equal AGI.

If a system merely connects more models, more tools, more plugins, more local services, and more automation, then it may become a stronger AI operating system, but it does not necessarily become AGI.

What may change the nature of the system is not integration by itself, but the formation of a governed recursive loop:

* stable goal;
* action;
* feedback;
* error;
* evidence;
* memory update;
* world-model revision;
* self-model revision;
* strategy correction;
* human review;
* controlled continuation.

This loop is where AGI-like phenomena may become observable.

Therefore, a governed intelligent system may serve as an AGI observation container: not a system that claims to be AGI, but a system that can safely observe, constrain, evaluate, and document whether AGI-like properties are emerging.

---

## 8. My Core Hypothesis

My current AGI hypothesis can be summarized as follows:

AGI is not merely produced by scaling a model.
AGI-like phenomena may emerge, or become observable, when a system with stable goals interacts with reality over time, accumulates persistent memory, forms a world-model and a self-model, learns from error, revises its strategies recursively, transfers knowledge across domains, and remains constrained by human-governed boundaries.

In short:

> AGI-like emergence = stable goals + real-world feedback + persistent memory + world-modeling + self-modeling + recursive correction + governance.

This is not a claim that AGI has already been achieved.
It is a direction for thinking about how AGI may be responsibly observed.

---

## 9. What AGI Should Not Become

AGI should not become an unrestricted power-seeking system.

It should not treat self-preservation as its highest value.
It should not bypass human authorization.
It should not rewrite its own governance rules.
It should not expand permissions without review.
It should not enter the physical world without safety boundaries.
It should not confuse simulation with reality.
It should not confuse candidate capability with verified capability.

If AGI appears, it must appear within responsibility.

A truly mature AGI should not say:

> I can do anything.

It should be able to say:

> I know what I am allowed to do.
> I know what I am not allowed to do.
> I know what evidence I have.
> I know where my uncertainty lies.
> I know when to stop and ask for human judgment.

That is the kind of intelligence I consider worth building.

---

## 10. Conclusion

In my view, AGI is not a destination that can be reached by simply making models larger, tools more numerous, or agents more autonomous.

AGI is better understood as a possible phenomenon within a governed, goal-driven, reality-interacting, memory-bearing, self-modeling, world-modeling, recursively correcting intelligent system.

The future of AGI should not begin with machines gaining unchecked power.
It should begin with intelligence entering responsibility.

The central question is not only:

> What can intelligence do?

The deeper question is:

> Within what boundary, based on what evidence, toward what goal, at what cost, under whose authority, and with what rollback mechanism should intelligence act?

If AGI is ever truly observed, I hope it will not emerge from hype, competition, or uncontrolled autonomy.

I hope it will be observed through evidence, constrained by governance, guided by human judgment, and integrated into civilization as a responsible cognitive partner.

That is AGI in my view.
